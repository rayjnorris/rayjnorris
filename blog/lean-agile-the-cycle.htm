LEAN AGILE - the cycle

March 21, 2017 (last updated March 22, 2017)

Lean Agile involves iterating short cycles of development, each contributing more clarity to the final solution, with the customer very active in ensuring correctness of the solution. This 'iterative' approach of short cycles of development and delivery is call Lean Agile. Does it work? Yes. However, take this to the bank: It is imperative that the customer be an active member of the team at all times. It’s that important. No customer? No Lean Agile.


A team-mate said to me; ‘I was shocked when you asked me how long a feature would take me create; and when I replied '4 hours', you said, 'good, then we can push it out today'.

I was practicing part of Lean. That is; to get something out 'there' (in this case UAT) as quickly as possible. Make something small and 'incremental' and push it! "Welcome to Lean Agile!"


Why use the 'lean agile' approach?

Because it makes happy people and makes successful solutions. Lean Development, borrowed from the Lean Production techniques practiced by the Toyota Production System, is being adopted by many companies today. At it's core, 'lean agile' is the concept of 'doing as little work as possible to achieve the greatest benefit as quickly possible, over and over again so that the final solution matures over time to the point of ‘value saturation’.


Key Success Drivers

  1. Evolve to Saturation - a key success driver is to evolve the solution in small chunks constantly until the customer feels there is really little else needed that warrants further development cost (value saturation is reached). Live with it a while. Repeat if warranted.
  2. Increase Speed / Reduce Cost - another key success driver is to keep speed up and costs low. How fast? Once a week pushes feel right in most cases, however multiple times a week is great in some cases. Longer than a week and things are getting a little slow for my taste.
  3. Customer Participation - as mentioned above, the customer (I mean the final user and all of the stakeholders in between) must have skin in the game; must be actively involved in envisioning the solution, costing it, prioritizing it, testing it, and signing off on push. The customer must feel (and be) in control of what is created .. after all, they're paying for it.   ** note: see article on 'the-customer''.

I earned the reputation for pushing teams to port solutions as quickly as I could get them to without inciting a revolt. (I incited a few revolts) I did this for many reasons, one of which was to reduce the cost and increase the speed of realizing clarity.
